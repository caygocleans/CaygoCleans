The only real solution is to find the source and eliminate it. Once the source is gone, the smell is gone — for good.

## [1:00 – 2:30] THE KITCHEN

[B-roll: Kitchen bin, drain, fridge, dish sponge, cutting board. Each highlighted as the narration mentions it.]

**NARRATION:**
"The kitchen is the most common source of bad smells in the home. And there are usually several things happening at once.

The bin. Even if you empty it regularly, the bin itself harbours bacteria and old food residue. Once a week, take the bin outside, rinse it with hot soapy water, and let it dry completely before putting a new bag in. A sprinkle of baking soda at the bottom of the bag absorbs odours between cleans.

The drain. Pour half a cup of baking soda down the kitchen drain, followed by half a cup of white vinegar. Let it fizz for five minutes, then flush with boiling water. Do this once a week. It breaks down the organic buildup that causes that sour drain smell.

The fridge. Old food, uncovered leftovers, and spills inside the fridge all produce odour that seeps out every time you open the door. Remove everything, wipe all surfaces with a solution of water and white vinegar, and place an open box of baking soda inside to continuously absorb odours.

The sponge. Your dish sponge may be the most bacteria-dense object in your entire home. It smells because it is full of decomposing food and moisture. Replace it every two weeks, or microwave it damp for 60 seconds to kill bacteria between replacements.

The cutting board. Especially wooden ones. Bacteria and food residue sink into the surface. Scrub with coarse salt and half a lemon — the salt exfoliates, the lemon kills bacteria and neutralises odour."

---

## [2:30 – 3:45] THE BATHROOM

[B-roll: Bathroom drain, toilet base, damp towels, bathroom bin, shower curtain.]

**NARRATION:**
"The bathroom has a few specific culprits that most people overlook.

The drain. Hair, soap scum, and moisture create a biofilm that produces a strong sulphur-like smell. Pull out any visible hair manually — unpleasant but necessary — then treat the drain with baking soda and vinegar weekly.

The toilet base. People clean the bowl but often miss the base and the floor around it. Urine splashes accumulate around the base of the toilet and produce a persistent smell that no amount of bowl cleaner will fix. Wipe the entire base and the surrounding floor at every clean.

Towels. Damp towels that aren't fully drying between uses develop mildew — that musty smell you might notice. Hang them spread open, not folded over a rail, and wash them at least once a week.

The bathroom bin. Small bins in bathrooms are often forgotten. Empty it often and wipe it down with an antibacterial spray weekly.

The shower curtain. Mildew grows on shower curtains faster than almost anything else in the bathroom. Wash it in the washing machine monthly or spray with a diluted white vinegar solution after every shower."

---

## [3:45 – 4:45] THE BEDROOM

[B-roll: Pillow, mattress, carpet, wardrobe, laundry pile.]

**NARRATION:**
"Bedrooms smell for different reasons — usually things people don't think to clean.

The mattress. You sweat in your sleep every single night. Over time, that moisture and body oil soaks into the mattress. Sprinkle baking soda over the entire mattress surface, leave for an hour, then vacuum it off. Do this every three to four months. Rotate your mattress at the same time.

Pillows. Pillows should be washed every three months. Most people never wash them. If yours have yellowed, that's sweat and oil buildup — they can still be washed in a machine on a gentle cycle.

Laundry. A pile of worn clothes sitting in a corner is a slow-release odour source. Use a lidded laundry basket and wash clothes regularly.

The wardrobe. Clothes packed too tightly in a closed wardrobe don't breathe, and the smell builds up. Leave a small dish of baking soda in the wardrobe and open it for ventilation regularly."

---

## [4:45 – 5:30] THE LIVING ROOM

[B-roll: Sofa cushions being lifted, carpet, curtains, remote controls, aircon vent.]

**NARRATION:**
"Living rooms trap odours in soft furnishings — and most people don't realise it.

The sofa. Fabric sofas absorb body odour, food smells, and pet smells over time. Sprinkle baking soda over the entire surface, leave for 30 minutes, then vacuum thoroughly. For a deeper refresh, a light spray of diluted white vinegar allowed to dry naturally works well.

Curtains. Fabric curtains act like a giant air filter — they trap dust and odours constantly. Wash them every three to six months.

The carpet. If you have carpet, it's likely holding more odour than anything else in the room. Baking soda sprinkled and left for an hour before vacuuming makes a significant difference.

The aircon. An aircon that hasn't had its filter cleaned is blowing dusty, stale air into your home constantly. Clean the filter monthly — we have a full guide on this separately."

---

## [5:30 – 6:15] THE WASHING MACHINE

[B-roll: Inside of a front-loading washing machine. Gasket/door seal highlighted. Running a hot cycle.]

**NARRATION:**
"This one surprises most people: your washing machine might be making your clothes smell worse, not better.

Front-loading machines especially are prone to mildew buildup in the rubber door seal — that ring of rubber around the door. Pull it back and look. If you see black or grey residue, that's mould — and every wash cycle pushes that smell onto your clothes.

Wipe the seal with a cloth dampened with white vinegar. Then run an empty hot cycle with two cups of white vinegar or a washing machine cleaning tablet.

After every wash, leave the door open to let the drum dry out. This single habit prevents mould from building up in the first place."

---

## [6:15 – 7:00] THE LONG-TERM SOLUTION: VENTILATION

[B-roll: Window being opened. Fresh air. Light breeze through curtains.]

**NARRATION:**
"Every solution I've mentioned targets a specific source — and that's important. But the single biggest factor in whether a home smells fresh or stale is ventilation.

A home that doesn't breathe traps odours, moisture, and stale air. Especially in Singapore where we tend to keep everything closed for the aircon.

Make a habit of opening windows for at least 15 minutes a day, even briefly. Cross-ventilation — opening windows on opposite sides of the home — clears air faster than any product.

In rooms with no windows, an exhaust fan or a small desk fan aimed at the door helps move air out.

Fresh air is free and it's the most effective deodoriser you'll ever find."

---

## [7:00 – 7:45] NATURAL VS CHEMICAL SOLUTIONS

[B-roll: Baking soda, white vinegar, lemon. Then: commercial sprays and plugins for comparison.]

**NARRATION:**
"A quick note on products. The market is full of plugins, sprays, gels, and diffusers that promise to keep your home smelling fresh.

Most of them mask odours. They don't eliminate them.

The three things I recommend keeping for odour control are: white vinegar — it neutralises alkaline odours rather than masking them. Baking soda — it absorbs acidic odours passively wherever you place it. And activated charcoal bags — these are excellent in enclosed spaces like wardrobes, cars, and shoe racks.

Essential oil diffusers are fine as a final touch once the source is eliminated. Not as a substitute."

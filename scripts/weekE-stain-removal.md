## [0:20 – 1:00] WHY SPEED AND TECHNIQUE MATTER

[B-roll: Close-up of a stain soaking into fabric fibres in slow motion.]

**NARRATION:**
"Here's what most people don't know about stains: they have a window.

In the first few minutes, most stains are sitting on the surface. They haven't bonded to the fibres or material yet. Act in that window and they come out easily.

Wait an hour? The stain starts to set. Wait a day? Some of them become permanent.

Speed matters. But technique matters more. Because even a fresh stain handled incorrectly can be driven so deep into a surface that no product on earth will fully remove it.

So before we go stain by stain, there are two rules that apply to every single stain on every single surface."

## [1:00 – 1:45] THE TWO UNIVERSAL RULES

[B-roll: Someone gently blotting a stain with a cloth — not rubbing. Text on screen appears.]

**NARRATION:**
"Rule one: blot, never rub.

Rubbing spreads the stain and pushes it deeper. Always press straight down with your cloth and lift — don't drag. Work from the outside of the stain inward so you're containing it, not spreading it.

Rule two: always test first.

Before applying any cleaning solution to a visible surface, test it on a hidden area — the back of the fabric, under the cushion, behind the furniture. Some surfaces and materials react badly to certain cleaners. A 30-second test can save you from ruining something permanently.

Blot, never rub. Test first. Everything else builds on these two rules."

## [1:45 – 3:15] COFFEE & TEA STAINS

[B-roll: Coffee stain on a white fabric surface. Blotting. Applying solution. Clean result.]

**NARRATION:**
"Coffee and tea are among the most common stains in any home — and fortunately, one of the easiest to remove if you're fast.

On fabric or carpet:
Blot up as much liquid as possible immediately. Then mix one tablespoon of washing-up liquid with one tablespoon of white vinegar and two cups of cold water. Apply the solution to the stain, blot, and repeat until the stain lifts. Rinse with cold water and blot dry.

Important: always use cold water for coffee and tea. Hot water sets the tannins in the stain and makes it significantly harder to remove.

On hard surfaces like countertops or tiles:
A simple wipe with an all-purpose cleaner is usually enough if caught quickly. For dried coffee rings, a paste of baking soda and a few drops of water, left for two minutes, then wiped away, works every time."

---

## [3:15 – 4:30] GREASE & OIL STAINS

[B-roll: Grease splatter on a stovetop. Applying dish soap. Wiping clean.]

**NARRATION:**
"Grease stains are different from most stains because water alone makes them worse. Water and oil don't mix — so adding water to a grease stain just spreads it.

On fabric or upholstery:
Sprinkle baking soda or cornstarch directly onto the stain and leave it for at least 15 minutes. It absorbs the oil. Then brush it off gently, apply a small amount of washing-up liquid directly to the stain, and work it in gently with your fingers. Rinse with warm — not hot — water.

On hard surfaces like stovetops, tiles, or countertops:
Washing-up liquid is your best friend. Apply it directly, let it sit for 30 seconds, then wipe. For baked-on grease — the kind that builds up over weeks on a stovetop — a paste of baking soda and washing-up liquid left for five minutes will break it down without scratching the surface."

---

## [4:30 – 5:30] BLOOD STAINS

[B-roll: A cloth being treated with cold water and salt solution.]

**NARRATION:**
"The most important thing to know about blood stains: cold water only. Never hot.

Heat causes the proteins in blood to coagulate — essentially cooking the stain into the fabric. This is irreversible.

For fresh blood on fabric:
Rinse immediately under cold running water to flush out as much as possible. Then apply a paste of salt and cold water, leave for a few minutes, and rinse again. For stubborn marks, a small amount of hydrogen peroxide applied to white or light fabrics will lift the stain. Test on coloured fabrics first — hydrogen peroxide can bleach.

For dried blood:
Soak the area in cold water for 30 minutes first to rehydrate the stain. Then treat as above. It will take more time and repetition, but it will come out."

---

## [5:30 – 6:30] RED WINE & JUICE STAINS

[B-roll: Red wine spill. Immediate blotting. Salt being applied. Clean result.]

**NARRATION:**
"Red wine has a reputation for being permanent. It doesn't have to be — if you act immediately.

Blot up as much wine as possible first. Then pour salt generously over the stain while it's still wet. The salt draws the wine out of the fabric. Leave for a few minutes, then brush away and treat with cold water and a small amount of washing-up liquid.

Club soda is another excellent immediate treatment — the carbonation helps lift the stain before it sets.

For dried red wine:
Mix one part dish soap with one part hydrogen peroxide. Apply to the stain, leave for 20 minutes, then rinse with cold water. Repeat if needed. This combination is highly effective but test on coloured fabrics first."

---

## [6:30 – 7:15] INK STAINS

[B-roll: Ink mark on fabric being treated with rubbing alcohol.]

**NARRATION:**
"Ink stains need a solvent, not water. Water alone will only spread them.

On fabric:
Place a clean cloth under the stained area. Apply rubbing alcohol — isopropyl alcohol — to the stain using a cotton ball, and blot from the outside in. The ink will transfer to your cotton ball. Keep using fresh cotton balls as they pick up the ink. Follow with washing-up liquid and cold water to remove the alcohol residue.

On hard surfaces like walls or desks:
Rubbing alcohol works here too. Apply with a cloth and wipe gently. For painted walls, be careful — alcohol can strip paint. Use a small amount and test in a hidden area first."

---

## [7:15 – 8:00] MOULD & MILDEW STAINS

[B-roll: Grout lines in a bathroom with mould. Applying solution. Scrubbing. Clean result.]

**NARRATION:**
"Mould stains are common in bathrooms and anywhere moisture collects — especially here in Singapore's humidity.

On tile grout:
Mix equal parts white vinegar and water in a spray bottle. Spray onto the grout, leave for 10 minutes, then scrub with an old toothbrush. For heavier mould, a paste of baking soda and water left for 15 minutes before scrubbing works even better.

On fabric or silicone sealant:
White vinegar or a diluted bleach solution — one part bleach to ten parts water — will kill mould on non-porous surfaces. Never use bleach on fabric unless it's white. For coloured fabric, white vinegar is safer.

Important: always ventilate the room when dealing with mould, and address the moisture source or the mould will return."

---

## [8:00 – 9:00] SURFACE-SPECIFIC QUICK RULES

[B-roll: Quick cuts of different surfaces — carpet, hardwood floor, marble countertop, fabric sofa.]

**NARRATION:**
"Different surfaces have different rules. Here's a quick reference.

Carpet: always blot, never scrub. Use cold water for most stains. Avoid over-wetting — too much moisture can cause mould underneath.

Hardwood floors: use as little liquid as possible. Excess water warps wood. Wipe spills immediately and dry thoroughly.

Marble and natural stone: never use vinegar or acidic cleaners. Acid etches the surface permanently. Use only pH-neutral cleaners on marble.

Upholstery and fabric sofas: always check the care label first. 'W' means water-based cleaners only. 'S' means solvent-based only. 'WS' means either. 'X' means vacuum only — no liquids.

White surfaces: hydrogen peroxide is safe and effective for most white surfaces — fabric, grout, and tiles."

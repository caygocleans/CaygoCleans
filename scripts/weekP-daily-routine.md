## [0:20 – 1:00] WHY DAILY BEATS WEEKLY

[B-roll: A clean home at the start of the week gradually becoming messy by the weekend. Then a home that stays consistently tidy.]

**NARRATION:**
"Most people clean reactively. They let things build up until the mess becomes uncomfortable, then spend a whole Saturday trying to dig out from under it. That's exhausting, demoralising, and it's why people feel like they can never keep up.

The daily routine flips this completely. When you do a little every day, nothing ever builds up enough to become a problem.

Think of it like this: brushing your teeth takes two minutes twice a day. That's why you don't need a dentist every week. Your home works the same way. Small daily maintenance prevents the big build-up.

The 20 minutes I'm about to give you is split into three parts of your day — morning, midday, and evening. None of them will feel like cleaning. They'll feel like a reset."

---

## [1:00 – 3:00] PART 1 — THE MORNING RESET (5 MINUTES)

[B-roll: Alarm going off. Bed being made. Bathroom sink being wiped. Kitchen counter cleared.]

**NARRATION:**
"The morning reset takes five minutes and sets the tone for your entire day.

Task one: make your bed. This takes 90 seconds and it matters more than you think. A made bed makes the entire bedroom look tidy, and psychologically it signals that the day has started intentionally. It's a small win that sets a standard for the rest of the day.

Task two: wipe the bathroom sink. After you brush your teeth and wash your face, take your damp face towel and give the sink basin and tap a quick wipe. 30 seconds. Toothpaste and water droplets that dry on a sink take much more effort to remove later. Caught immediately, they wipe off with no effort.

Task three: clear the kitchen counter. Any cups, plates, or items left out from the night before — put them away or into the sink. A clear counter makes the kitchen feel clean even before it's been properly cleaned.

That's your morning. Five minutes. Bed made, bathroom wiped, kitchen counter clear."

[On-screen text: Morning tasks listed with checkboxes]

---

## [3:00 – 4:45] PART 2 — THE MIDDAY TIDY (3 MINUTES)

[B-roll: Dishes being rinsed after a meal. Kitchen wiped down quickly. Cushions straightened.]

**NARRATION:**
"The midday tidy is three minutes — ideally done right after lunch.

Task one: deal with dishes immediately. The biggest mess that accumulates in any home is dishes left in the sink. One plate and one cup after lunch — rinse them and put them in the dish rack or dishwasher now. This takes 60 seconds. Left until evening, the kitchen feels heavy and the task feels bigger than it is.

Task two: wipe the stovetop and counter after cooking. If you cooked anything, give the stovetop and counter a quick wipe with a damp cloth. 90 seconds while everything is still loose and easy to remove. Do this after every meal you cook and your kitchen will never need a deep clean.

Task three: a 30-second reset of the living area. Straighten any cushions, fold any throws, and put anything out of place back where it belongs.

Three minutes. Done."

[On-screen text: Midday tasks with checkboxes]

---

## [4:45 – 7:15] PART 3 — THE EVENING RESET (12 MINUTES)

[B-roll: Dishes being done after dinner. Kitchen wiped down. Floor being quickly vacuumed. Surfaces straightened. Bathroom given a quick wipe.]

**NARRATION:**
"The evening reset is the main event — 12 minutes that closes out the day and means you wake up to a clean home tomorrow.

Task one: clear and wash up after dinner. Five minutes. Dishes done, pots cleaned, stovetop wiped, counters cleared. The kitchen is the heart of the home and ending the day with it clean makes the whole home feel resolved.

Task two: a quick vacuum or sweep of the main living areas. Three minutes. You don't need to move furniture — just run the vacuum or a dry mop over the kitchen floor, living room floor, and hallway. These are the high-traffic areas that collect visible debris during the day. Your cordless vacuum is perfect for this — it should take under three minutes.

Task three: bathroom reset. Two minutes. Wipe the sink basin, straighten anything on the counter, put the toilet lid down, hang any towels properly. You're not deep cleaning — you're just resetting the room back to its baseline.

Task four: a 2-minute walk-through of the whole home. Move from room to room and put anything out of place back where it belongs — clothes on chairs, items on the floor, anything that's drifted. This is the most important task of the evening because it means you wake up to order rather than chaos.

Twelve minutes total. The home is reset. You can rest."

[On-screen text: Evening tasks with checkboxes and times]

---

## [7:15 – 8:15] THE WEEKLY TOP-UP (WHAT STILL NEEDS TO BE DONE)

[B-roll: Bathroom being properly cleaned. Floors being mopped. Bins being emptied.]

**NARRATION:**
"The daily routine keeps your home looking and feeling clean. But some tasks still need to happen weekly to maintain the standard.

Once a week, pick one day for these:

Empty all bins in the home. Properly clean the bathroom — toilet, shower, and floors. Mop the hard floors rather than just vacuuming or sweeping. Change the bed sheets. Wipe down surfaces — kitchen cabinets, light switches, door handles.

This weekly session, because you've been maintaining daily, now takes 30 to 45 minutes instead of several hours. You're not catching up — you're just doing the things daily resets don't cover.

Daily resets plus one weekly session. That's the entire system."

---

## [8:15 – 9:00] HOW TO MAKE IT STICK

[B-roll: Morning routine showing the tasks being done efficiently and calmly. Not rushed.]

**NARRATION:**
"The hardest part of any routine isn't doing it — it's starting it. Here's how to make this one stick.

Habit stacking: attach each task to something you already do. Make your bed immediately after waking up, before you check your phone. Wipe the bathroom sink as the last step of your morning routine. Wipe the kitchen counter as part of cooking — not after.

The two-minute rule: if something takes less than two minutes, do it now. Don't let it sit. Dishes after every meal. Wipe after every cook. Put things back immediately. These micro-habits are what keep the system running.

And lower the bar on hard days. Some days 20 minutes won't happen. That's fine. Even five minutes — just the bed and the kitchen counter — maintains enough momentum to start again tomorrow. A 20-minute routine done imperfectly beats a perfect routine done never."
